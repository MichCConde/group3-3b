Case Studies of Successful Ecotechnology Implementations

EcoHealth

Solar-Powered Hospitals:

Facilities that have cut costs and emissions while improving patient care.


Castle Hill Hospital, Hull, UK
  • The Castle Hill Hospital, part of the Hull University Teaching Hospitals NHS Trust, installed 11,000 solar panels, creating one of the first entirely solar-powered hospitals in the UK.
  • This installation generates around 26 MWh of power daily, expected to peak at 50 MWh during summer. The project is anticipated to save the hospital approximately £250,000 to £300,000 each month on energy bills, while also contributing significantly to the hospital’s goal of becoming carbon neutral by 2030​
    • CentraState Medical Center, New Jersey, USA
  • CentraState Medical Center has partnered with KDC Solar to develop a ground-mounted photovoltaic solar project on its campus.
  • This 7 MW solar farm supplies up to 30% of the hospital's electricity needs, saving about $300,000 annually in energy costs. ​
  • The hospital benefits from fixed solar power costs, enabling long-term budget forecasting and reducing dependency on volatile energy markets​
    • Makunda Christian Leprosy and General Hospital, Assam, India
  • Located in a remote area with frequent power outages, this hospital has integrated solar power to enhance its energy reliability.
  • The use of solar energy, supplemented by diesel generators during extended outages, has significantly improved the hospital's operational stability, ensuring that critical services remain uninterrupted. ​
  • This hybrid approach underscores the potential of solar power in improving healthcare delivery in rural settings​ ​
    • San Benito Health Foundation, California, USA
  • This clinic implemented a $1.7 million solar and battery microgrid system, allowing it to operate almost entirely on solar power.
  • This self-contained system provides the clinic with the ability to function independently of the grid for up to a week, ensuring continuity of care during outages and emergencies. ​
  • The initiative has been particularly beneficial for the clinic's low-income, largely Hispanic community​

    • Biodegradable Medical Supplies: Hospitals reducing waste and minimizing environmental harm.


    Erasmus University Medical Center, Netherlands
  • Erasmus MC is among the hospitals that have adopted the Pharmafilter system, which processes all hospital waste, including biodegradable materials, through anaerobic digestion.
  • This system not only simplifies waste management but also generates biogas, reducing the hospital's carbon footprint and enhancing sustainability efforts. The switch to biodegradable disposables, such as bedpans and containers made from bio-based plastics, has further reduced the volume of non-degradable waste​
    • Stanford Medical Center, USA
  • Stanford Medical Center conducted a pilot study to recycle plastic medical supplies and has since integrated a comprehensive waste management program.
  • By focusing on clean, dry recyclable materials, primarily plastics, Stanford diverted approximately 110 tons of waste from landfills annually. ​
  • The program includes using biodegradable and recyclable materials, and it highlights the financial benefits of waste diversion, achieving significant cost savings​ ​
    • Pharmafilter System in Dutch Hospitals
  • This innovative system installed in multiple Dutch hospitals, including Reinier de Graaf Hospital and Zaans Medical Center, treats both solid and liquid hospital waste.
  • It includes a high-tech shredder and anaerobic digestion unit that processes biodegradable disposables, leading to reduced environmental impact and improved waste management efficiency. ​
  • This system also contributes to lowering hospital-acquired infections and combating antibiotic resistance by efficiently treating contaminated waste​
    • Bio-based Products in European Hospitals
  • Research conducted by Wageningen University & Research has identified several biodegradable disposable products already in use or available for hospitals.
  • These include items like paper cups, tissues, and disposable clothing made from cellulose fibers. Hospitals are encouraged to switch to bio-based versions of these products to take advantage of their biodegradability and reduce waste sent to landfills or incineration​

    • Urban Farming Initiatives: Clinics offering fresh, organic produce to patients, improving nutrition and health outcomes.


    Baltimore, Maryland, USA
  • An urban farm-corner store collaboration in Baltimore aimed to increase access to fresh produce in low-income neighborhoods. The initiative paired urban farms with local corner stores, ensuring that residents had convenient access to fresh fruits and vegetables.
  • During the intervention, stores involved in the program saw significant increases in the variety and quantity of produce sold.​
  • The success of the initiative in one store was attributed to strong community support and effective promotion strategies, which included tastings and recipe cards to familiarize customers with the produce. This model demonstrated the potential to improve fruit and vegetable intake among community members by leveraging existing retail infrastructure​​
    • Fenway Farms
  • Fenway Park's rooftop garden, Fenway Farms, was established in 2015 and yields between 4,000 and 6,000 pounds of fresh produce each year. The garden, maintained by Green City Growers, produces a variety of vegetables and herbs, which are used in the park's restaurants and concession stands.
  • This farm-to-table approach ensures that fans and staff receive fresh, organic food while reducing the ballpark's reliance on external food suppliers. ​
  • The initiative also includes educational components, with tours and visibility to hundreds of thousands of visitors annually, promoting awareness about urban agriculture and sustainability​ ​​
    • Boston Medical Center Rooftop Farm
  • Boston Medical Center (BMC) launched its rooftop farm in 2017, becoming the first hospital in Boston to integrate such a green space.
  • This 5,000 square foot garden produces a variety of organic vegetables and herbs, contributing thousands of pounds of fresh produce annually.​
  • The produce supports the hospital's Preventive Food Pantry, which provides food to patients in need, and is also used in patient meals and the hospital's demonstration kitchen. The garden features a modular raised-bed system with efficient drip irrigation, enhancing its sustainability.​​
  • This initiative not only supplies nutritious food but also reduces the hospital’s carbon footprint by absorbing stormwater and lowering energy use​
